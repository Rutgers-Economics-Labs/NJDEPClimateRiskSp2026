Climate Risk, Resilience Signaling, and Municipal Bond Spreads in New Jersey

Abstract
This paper evaluates whether climate exposure and resilience-program participation are associated with municipal bond spreads in New Jersey. The current repository supports an exploratory analysis built from WRDS secondary-market municipal trades, a deterministic synthetic AAA benchmark, municipality-level sea-level-rise exposure metrics, and resilience indicators derived from available NJ CHAMP applicant materials and related controls. The main estimating strategy combines pooled OLS, two-way fixed effects difference-in-differences, a coastal triple-difference specification, an event-study-style pre-trend diagnostic, and a municipality-level AIPW-DiD sensitivity check. The headline empirical result is narrow: the coastal triple interaction between CHAMP cohort status, the post-2022 period, and 4-foot sea-level-rise exposure is positive but statistically indistinguishable from zero. Although the basic two-way fixed effects model estimates lower post-2022 spreads for CHAMP-cohort municipalities, the pre-treatment trend test and limited overlap in the treated sample weaken a policy-causal interpretation. For NJDEP, the most defensible conclusion is not that resilience investment is currently rewarded or penalized by the bond market, but that the present data infrastructure is not yet strong enough to support a final policy-grade causal estimate of borrowing-cost effects.

Presentation-Ready Summary
What this paper can defend tomorrow is straightforward. The project successfully builds an integrated New Jersey panel linking municipal bond trades, tax-base-weighted sea-level-rise exposure, CHAMP cohort indicators, and fiscal controls. In that panel, the climate-specific triple-difference result is not statistically significant, while the simpler post-2022 CHAMP spread-compression result is weakened by meaningful pre-trend and overlap concerns. The correct message for NJDEP is therefore that this is a strong exploratory analysis and a credible prototype for a future policy-grade study, but not yet a final causal estimate of resilience effects on borrowing costs.

1. Introduction
New Jersey has strong practical reasons to ask whether climate risk is priced into local public finance. Municipalities facing repeated tidal flooding, chronic drainage failures, and long-run sea-level-rise exposure may encounter higher expected infrastructure costs, weaker tax-base durability, and heightened disclosure pressure. If investors price those risks, climate exposure should appear in municipal bond spreads. If adaptation programs credibly reduce expected losses or improve institutional capacity, those same markets could in principle reward resilient municipalities with tighter spreads.

This paper studies that question using the current project repository prepared for the New Jersey Department of Environmental Protection (NJDEP). The original project scope proposed a 2015-2025 New Jersey panel linking bond premia, flood exposure, resilience indicators, and controls, with a focus on a post-2022 policy shift tied to resilience financing and project prioritization. The implementation now in the codebase achieves much of that integration, but it does so with an exploratory secondary-market trade panel and a synthetic AAA benchmark rather than validated primary-market issuance data and a market-standard MMD or Bloomberg benchmark. That distinction matters. The current analysis is useful as a screening exercise, but it should not be presented as a final causal estimate of borrowing costs.

The paper’s contribution is therefore narrower and more practical than the original ambition. First, it documents the actual analytical panel produced by the code, including the treatment flags, climate-risk measure, and control variables that survive the full merge process. Second, it reports the corrected regression and AIPW sensitivity results now contained in the repository. Third, it translates those results into a policy-facing conclusion appropriate for NJDEP: there is no robust statistical evidence in the current dataset that high-exposure CHAMP-cohort municipalities were penalized after 2022, and the evidence is not yet strong enough to claim that the policy shift produced spread compression either.

2. Policy and Institutional Background
The policy motivation for this project comes from the interaction of three New Jersey systems. The first is the climate-risk system: New Jersey’s coastal and tidal municipalities face material exposure to sea-level rise and related flooding hazards, and NJDEP guidance increasingly requires decision-makers to incorporate climate-adjusted conditions into planning and infrastructure decisions. The second is the infrastructure-finance system: local governments and utilities rely on public borrowing and state-supported financing channels to fund water, stormwater, and resilience-related capital improvements. The third is the resilience-governance system: programs such as FEMA’s Community Rating System (CRS), municipal stormwater management requirements, and New Jersey’s CHAMP-related prioritization frameworks attempt to identify or incentivize risk-reducing action.

The original project scope treated the 2022 policy environment as a meaningful pivot point. The working assumption in both the slide deck and the code is that July 1, 2022, the start of State Fiscal Year 2023, is the relevant post period for testing whether resilience-linked municipalities experienced differential spread movement. In the repository, that timing enters as the binary variable `is_post_2022`. CHAMP participation is represented in two ways: a time-invariant cohort flag, `ever_champ`, and a time-varying indicator, `is_resilient`, which switches on beginning with the municipality’s first observed CHAMP state fiscal year. Because the present CHAMP treatment file is built from locally parsed applicant materials rather than a fully validated historical program record, the paper treats those variables as informative but provisional.

This institutional context implies several competing channels. Resilience designation could signal stronger preparedness, lower expected losses, and better future credit quality. But the same designation could also reveal risk, identify municipalities under fiscal or infrastructure stress, or correlate with places that already expected to pursue large capital programs. The empirical design must therefore separate resilience signaling from climate exposure and from pre-existing municipal differences. The current dataset only partially succeeds at that task, which is why interpretation needs to remain cautious.

3. Conceptual Framework
Municipal bond spreads can respond to climate risk through at least four mechanisms. First, investors may apply a physical-risk penalty when a larger share of the local tax base is exposed to inundation or recurring flood damage. Second, they may assign a resilience premium if credible adaptation lowers expected disruption, preserves collateral and tax-base stability, or improves institutional capacity. Third, resilience-program participation may work as a disclosure signal: participation can reveal risk as much as it reveals preparedness. Fourth, resilience investments may require near-term borrowing or operational commitments that temporarily weaken balance-sheet measures even if they improve long-run welfare.

These channels lead to testable but not mutually exclusive expectations. A positive association between `slr_exposure_pct` and spreads would be consistent with climate-risk pricing. A negative post-2022 CHAMP effect would be consistent with improved pricing for resilience-linked municipalities, but only if pre-treatment trends are parallel and treatment timing is correctly measured. A near-zero triple interaction between CHAMP status, the post period, and sea-level-rise exposure would suggest that the market is not differentially rewarding or penalizing resilience-linked coastal exposure in a way that is detectable in the current sample. The present results point most clearly toward that third outcome.

4. Data and Sample Construction
The final analytical panel is created by `data_processing/build_master_panel.py`, which merges cleaned bond trades, spatial risk measures, CHAMP indicators, MS4 stormwater data, fiscal controls, and ACS income controls into `data/data_cleaned/final_panel_master.csv`. That build script is the authoritative description of the implemented data pipeline and is more reliable than the earlier conceptual scope.

Bond market data come from WRDS municipal securities transactions and are filtered to New Jersey general-obligation-like municipal trades. The working paper deck refers to roughly 187,000 bond observations, but after the actual support restrictions used in the econometric scripts, the preferred panel contains 170,872 trade-level observations across 145 municipalities from 2015 through 2025, excluding 2020. The core dependent variable is `spread_bps`, defined as the traded bond yield minus a deterministic synthetic AAA benchmark and expressed in basis points. The repository’s own documentation correctly warns that this benchmark is not a substitute for a same-date, same-maturity Bloomberg or MMD AAA municipal curve.

Climate exposure comes from `data/data_cleaned/climate/nj_statewide_frm_scores_FINAL.csv`, merged as `slr_exposure_pct`. In the code, this variable is explicitly defined as `FRM_RATIO_4FT * 100`, meaning the percentage of municipal market value exposed under a 4-foot sea-level-rise scenario. This tax-base-weighted measure is a stronger proxy for economically meaningful exposure than simple flooded land area because municipal credit depends more directly on the durability of the taxable property base than on areal inundation alone.

Resilience indicators are drawn from several places, but the paper’s main treatment variable is the CHAMP cohort flag built from locally parsed CHAMP applicant files. The code preserves `ever_champ`, `first_champ_sfy`, `best_champ_rank`, `crs_resilient`, and the time-varying `is_resilient` flag. That is useful for future refinement, but the present analysis uses `ever_champ` as the main cohort marker in the difference-in-differences specifications. Additional municipality-level controls include `debt_to_av` from New Jersey UFB fiscal data and `median_income` from ACS data, with 2022 ACS values forward-filled in the repository for 2023-2025.

The preferred regression scripts further restrict the sample to municipalities with both pre- and post-2022 observations and winsorize spreads at the 1st and 99th percentiles. After that filter, the panel includes 145 municipalities, of which 15 are in the CHAMP cohort and 130 are not. The municipality-level AIPW-DiD sensitivity design collapses the panel to 145 municipalities as first differences in pre- and post-period average spreads.

5. Empirical Strategy
The empirical strategy follows the repository’s implemented scripts rather than the earlier conceptual proposal alone. The pooled baseline specification estimates whether CHAMP cohort status, sea-level-rise exposure, and a small set of controls are associated with winsorized spreads:

Spread_it = beta_0 + beta_1 CHAMP_i + beta_2 Post_t + beta_3 SLR_i + X_it gamma + e_it.

This model is descriptive. Because it does not absorb municipality fixed effects, it cannot separate climate-risk or CHAMP effects from persistent cross-town differences in wealth, credit quality, or bond-market composition.

The main panel specification is a two-way fixed effects difference-in-differences model:

Spread_it = alpha_i + lambda_t + beta (CHAMP_i x Post_t) + gamma TTM_it + e_it.

Here `alpha_i` are municipality fixed effects, `lambda_t` are year fixed effects, and `TTM_it` is time to maturity. In the implemented code, debt-to-assessed-value is intentionally omitted from this preferred fixed-effects model. The identifying assumption is that absent the post-2022 policy environment, CHAMP-cohort and non-CHAMP municipalities would have followed parallel trends in spreads after conditioning on the included controls and fixed effects.

The coastal triple-difference specification extends the model by interacting the post period and CHAMP status with continuous sea-level-rise exposure:

Spread_it = alpha_i + lambda_t + beta_1 (CHAMP_i x Post_t) + beta_2 (Post_t x SLR_i) + beta_3 (CHAMP_i x Post_t x SLR_i) + gamma TTM_it + e_it.

The parameter of interest is `beta_3`, interpreted as the incremental post-2022 spread change associated with one additional percentage point of 4-foot SLR exposure among CHAMP-cohort municipalities relative to the comparison group.

The repository also includes two diagnostics. First, `run_did_regression.py` estimates an event-study-style model using within-municipality demeaning and CHAMP-by-year interactions with 2021 as the omitted year. Second, `run_aipw_ate.py` implements a Sant’Anna and Zhao style doubly robust AIPW-DiD estimator at the municipality level using pre/post mean spread changes. These are not replacements for a stronger design, but they are useful checks on whether the headline fixed-effects result survives weaker overlap and pre-trend concerns.

Standard errors in the regression models are clustered by municipality throughout.

6. Results
The pooled OLS model does not show clear evidence that sea-level-rise exposure is priced in a simple cross-sectional way. The coefficient on `slr_exposure_pct` is positive but tiny, about 0.043 basis points per percentage point of exposure, with a p-value of 0.537. The `ever_champ` coefficient is negative, about -6.256 basis points, but statistically insignificant at conventional levels. In plain terms, once maturity, debt, income, and a post-2022 indicator are added, the pooled model does not reveal a robust stand-alone climate-risk premium or resilience discount.

The two-way fixed effects DiD model produces a much larger result. The interaction `ever_champ x post_2022` is approximately -32.625 basis points and statistically significant. Mechanically, that means CHAMP-cohort municipalities experienced more spread compression after 2022 than non-CHAMP municipalities in the fixed-effects panel. If this estimate stood on clean identification, it would be economically meaningful.

But the triple-difference model is the better climate-resilience test for NJDEP’s purposes. Its key coefficient, `ever_champ x post_2022 x slr_exposure_pct`, is about +0.187 basis points per additional percentage point of 4-foot SLR exposure, with a p-value of 0.497. This estimate is not statistically distinguishable from zero. The post-period interaction with SLR exposure alone is also not significant. That means the current data do not support a claim that higher-exposure CHAMP municipalities received a differentiated market response after 2022.

The strongest caution comes from the pre-trend diagnostics. The linear differential pre-trend for the CHAMP cohort is about -3.73 basis points per year with a p-value near 0.024. The event-study coefficients likewise show that CHAMP municipalities were already converging toward lower spreads before the post-2022 period. That pattern directly weakens the parallel-trends assumption needed to interpret the fixed-effects DiD coefficient causally. A policy audience should therefore not read the negative DiD estimate as proof that the post-2022 resilience policy caused borrowing-cost reductions.

7. AIPW-DiD Sensitivity Analysis
The municipality-level AIPW-DiD exercise is directionally consistent with the fixed-effects DiD result but materially less decisive. In `results/aipw_results.txt`, the naive difference in mean spread changes is about -11.55 basis points. The analytic AIPW estimate is about -13.09 basis points with a p-value of 0.0686, while the bootstrap AIPW interval is much wider and includes zero comfortably. This is exactly the kind of pattern that should trigger caution rather than confidence.

The AIPW diagnostics also reveal overlap and balance problems. CHAMP municipalities are much more exposed to sea-level rise and have lower median incomes on average than non-CHAMP municipalities. Weighting improves balance on some observables but does not remove all concern, and the effective sample size for treated municipalities is small. In a setting with only 15 treated municipalities, those diagnostics matter. They suggest that even a doubly robust estimator cannot fully rescue causal interpretation if treatment assignment is concentrated in a small, unusual subset of municipalities.

8. Discussion
The central policy point is that the current evidence is mixed, not null in every dimension but not robust enough to justify a strong causal narrative. One result, the fixed-effects DiD coefficient, points toward tighter post-2022 spreads for CHAMP municipalities. But the more climate-specific triple-difference term is null, the pre-trend test fails in a substantive sense, and the municipality-level AIPW exercise shows limited overlap and wide uncertainty. Taken together, these findings imply that the market may be reacting to some combination of municipality selection, changing composition of traded bonds, or broader municipal characteristics rather than to resilience policy itself.

That interpretation is also consistent with the structure of the underlying data. The dependent variable is based on secondary-market trades, not primary issuance yields. Secondary-market trading patterns reflect liquidity, dealer behavior, bond age, insurance, callability, issue structure, and investor clientele as well as underlying credit risk. In addition, the benchmark used in the current code is not a validated municipal AAA curve but a deterministic synthetic series. Those choices are acceptable for an exploratory prototype, but they materially limit the precision of any claim about municipal borrowing costs.

The paper therefore does not conclude that resilience programs fail to matter. It concludes that the current measurement system does not yet isolate their effect with enough credibility. That distinction is important for NJDEP. The absence of robust statistical evidence in this repository should not be read as evidence that resilience has no fiscal value. It should be read as evidence that the state needs better linked data before the market-pricing question can be answered cleanly.

9. Policy Implications for NJDEP
For NJDEP, the most valuable immediate implication is institutional rather than econometric. The state would benefit from a more formal data pipeline linking resilience project records, financing approvals, project completion dates, and municipal bond issuance characteristics. If the policy question is whether resilience programs affect the cost of capital, the state needs a panel that measures actual issuance spreads against a validated municipal benchmark and ties those spreads to verified treatment timing.

The second implication is communicative. NJDEP can still use this work as a high-quality exploratory screening analysis. The repository shows that a statewide panel can be assembled, that tax-base-weighted SLR exposure is a tractable explanatory variable, and that resilience indicators can be merged to market data. But the results should be framed as diagnostic associations rather than as proof of a resilience premium or a climate penalty.

The third implication is analytical. Future iterations should prioritize same-date, same-maturity MMD or Bloomberg AAA spreads, issuance rather than trade yields where possible, and richer bond-level controls including ratings, insurance, tax status, callability, trade size, issue size, and liquidity proxies. A validated CHAMP and Water Bank treatment history would also allow a cleaner event-time design with more defensible policy timing.

10. Limitations
Several limitations are binding in the current version of the project. First, the benchmark is synthetic, deterministic, and not a substitute for a true municipal AAA curve. Second, the analysis uses secondary-market trades rather than issuance or offering yields, so the dependent variable is not a direct measure of primary borrowing cost. Third, important bond-level controls are still missing. Fourth, CHAMP treatment timing is based on locally parsed applicant files rather than a complete administrative program history. Fifth, the pre-treatment trend test indicates that the treatment and comparison groups were already evolving differently before the policy pivot. Sixth, the final sample contains only 145 municipalities and 15 treated municipalities after support restrictions. Seventh, 2020 is absent from the bond panel. Each of these issues limits interpretability; together they require restraint in policy claims.

11. Path to a Policy-Grade Final Paper
If this project were extended after the presentation, the path to a fully defensible paper is clear. The first priority would be outcome validation: replacing the synthetic benchmark with same-date, same-maturity MMD or Bloomberg AAA benchmarks and, if possible, moving from secondary-market trade yields to issuance or offering yields. The second priority would be treatment validation: reconstructing an official municipality-by-year CHAMP, Water Bank, or I-Bank treatment file with exact approval dates, project categories, and implementation timing. The third priority would be model enrichment: adding rating, insurance, tax status, callability, issue size, trade size, and liquidity controls so that climate and resilience coefficients are not forced to absorb ordinary bond-market variation.

The fourth priority would be identification redesign. Because the current panel shows differential pre-trends, the next version should not rely primarily on the present two-way fixed effects specification. A stronger strategy could use validated event time, matched issuance comparisons, stacked difference-in-differences, or a design focused on clearly treated municipalities with cleaner support. The fifth priority would be a full robustness suite, including placebo treatment dates, alternative exposure thresholds, different resilience definitions, coastal-only subsamples, raw versus winsorized spreads, and sensitivity to influential municipalities.

Taken together, those steps would convert the current project from a strong exploratory screening paper into a policy-grade empirical study that could support firmer claims about whether resilience programs affect municipal financing costs.

12. Conclusion
The current New Jersey climate-risk bond panel does not support a strong claim that resilience-linked municipalities were systematically rewarded or penalized by the municipal bond market after 2022. The most climate-specific coefficient in the preferred triple-difference model is statistically indistinguishable from zero, and the key identifying assumption behind the simpler DiD result is weakened by detectable pre-treatment convergence. The best reading of the evidence is therefore modest: the project demonstrates a workable analytical architecture and surfaces several suggestive patterns, but it does not yet deliver a final causal estimate suitable for strong policy conclusions about borrowing-cost effects.

For NJDEP, that is still a useful result. It clarifies what can and cannot be defended with the present evidence, and it points directly toward the next improvements needed for a policy-grade analysis: validated benchmark yields, verified treatment timing, issuance-level data, and richer bond controls. Those investments would materially strengthen the state’s ability to quantify the fiscal value of resilience.
