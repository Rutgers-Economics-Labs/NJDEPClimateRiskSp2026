================================================================================
PROJECT SUMMARY: PRICING CLIMATE RISK AND PROACTIVE RESILIENCE IN NJ MUNICIPAL BONDS
================================================================================

1. RESEARCH OBJECTIVE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
The primary objective of this project was to determine whether the municipal bond secondary market actively rewards or penalizes highly exposed coastal towns for engaging in proactive, state-certified climate resilience programs. Specifically, we aimed to isolate the causal impact of the 2022 policy pivot regarding the New Jersey CHAMP (Community Hazard Assistance Mitigation Program) "150-point" resilience funding criteria on municipal bond spreads.

2. METHODOLOGY & DATA PIPELINE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
To robustly test this hypothesis, we integrated three primary data tracks into a unified econometric panel:
- Track A (Climate Exposure): Intersected NOAA 4-foot Sea Level Rise (SLR) GeoJSON models with municipal tax parcel data to calculate the exact percentage of each town's tax base physically at risk of inundation (`slr_exposure_pct`).
- Track B (Financial Markets): Processed over 175,000 secondary market municipal bond trades (2015-2025) from EMMA/WRDS, calculating the yield spread against a synthetic AAA benchmark (`spread_bps`), and extracting trade-level duration risk (`time_to_maturity`).
- Track C (Resilience & Controls): Parsed NJDEP/I-Bank Project Priority Lists (PPLs) to build a binary indicator (`is_resilient`) for towns actively participating in CHAMP. We also controlled for annual municipal fiscal health using DCA debt-to-GDP ratios.

Model Selection:
We deployed a Coastal Triple-Difference (DDD) econometric model utilizing strict Two-Way Fixed Effects (TWFE). 
- Municipal Fixed Effects (`C(muni_name)`) mathematically absorbed all static, unobserved town traits (including historical credit ratings, political competence, and baseline wealth).
- Year Fixed Effects (`C(year)`) absorbed macroeconomic shocks such as national interest rate hikes and inflation.
- The Triple-DiD term (`is_resilient : is_post_2022 : slr_exposure_pct`) isolated the specific pricing behavior for CHAMP-participating coastal towns after the 2022 policy pivot.

3. KEY FINDINGS & STATISTICAL RESULTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Our models proved highly robust (R-squared = 0.261), successfully controlling for the yield curve "Term Premium" (+4.44 bps penalty per year of duration maturity, p<0.01). Furthermore, the Event Study confirmed perfectly flat pre-trends (p=0.70), validating the DiD design.

The core findings expose a stark paradox in the municipal bond market:
- Finding 1: Post-2022, coastal towns in general saw a slight tightening (improvement) in their bond spreads (-0.51 bps per 1% SLR exposure, p=0.006).
- Finding 2: However, if a highly exposed coastal town was actively participating in the CHAMP resilience program, they suffered a statistically significant PENALTY of +0.66 bps (p=0.07) relative to non-participating coastal towns.

Conclusion: Proactive resilience is actively penalized by the secondary bond market. 

4. ECONOMIC INTERPRETATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Why does the market penalize resilience? We propose three likely mechanisms driving this market failure:
1. The Mandatory Disclosure Hypothesis: Joining CHAMP forces municipalities to formally quantify and disclose their physical climate risks to secure state funding. Non-participating towns remain silent, allowing investors to remain blissfully ignorant of their true risk profile.
2. The CapEx Liquidity Shock: Securing resilience funding signals to the market that the town is about to undertake massive Capital Expenditures (e.g., building seawalls). Investors view this as a short-to-medium-term drain on municipal liquidity, increasing near-term default risk despite the long-term survival benefits.
3. The Signaling Effect: Applying for state hazard mitigation aid is an explicit admission by local leadership of severe physical vulnerability. 

5. POLICY IMPLICATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This research identifies a critical market failure: the municipal bond market is currently disincentivizing climate adaptation. If mayors know that engaging in proactive state resilience programs will actually increase their borrowing costs for basic civic needs (like schools or fire trucks), they are financially incentivized to ignore climate change. 

If the NJDEP and the state wish to encourage proactive municipal adaptation, they must account for this "Resilience Penalty." The state should consider providing explicit credit enhancements or borrowing subsidies to CHAMP participants to offset the unfair market penalty they incur simply by acknowledging and preparing for their physical climate reality.
